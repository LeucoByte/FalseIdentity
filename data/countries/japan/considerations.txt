# CULTURAL CONSIDERATIONS FOR JAPAN

## Social Behavior
- Japanese society values harmony (wa) and avoiding direct confrontation
- Politeness and respect are deeply ingrained in all interactions
- Bowing is the standard greeting, with depth indicating level of respect
- Removing shoes when entering homes and certain establishments is mandatory
- Gift-giving (omiyage) is an important social custom, especially when visiting or traveling
- Punctuality is extremely important and being late is considered very rude

## Communication Style
- Indirect communication is preferred to maintain harmony
- Reading between the lines (kuuki wo yomu - reading the air) is expected
- Saying "no" directly is often avoided; refusals are expressed subtly
- Silence is valued and comfortable, not awkward
- Excessive eye contact can be seen as aggressive or disrespectful
- Business cards (meishi) are exchanged with both hands and treated with respect

## Work Culture
- Long working hours and dedication to company are common (especially for older generations)
- Lifetime employment system (shushin koyo) still influences older workers
- Hierarchical structure based on seniority (senpai-kohai system)
- After-work drinking sessions (nomikai) are important for building relationships
- Taking vacation days is often viewed negatively, though changing among younger workers
- Overtime (zangyou) is widespread and often unpaid

## Family and Social Structure
- Strong emphasis on family obligation and filial piety
- Multi-generational households were traditional, though less common now
- Respect for elders (keiro) is fundamental
- Gender roles are still relatively traditional, especially in older generations
- Marriage is often viewed as a social necessity, creating pressure (especially on women)
- Parent-child bond (especially mother-child) is very strong

## Daily Life
- Rice, fish, and miso soup are dietary staples
- Seasonal awareness (shun) influences food, activities, and aesthetics
- Public baths (sento) and hot springs (onsen) are important social activities
- Convenience stores (konbini) are integral to daily life, open 24/7
- Public transportation is extremely punctual and efficient
- Cash is still widely used despite technological advancement

## Etiquette and Customs
- Never tip - it can be considered insulting
- Slurping noodles is acceptable and shows appreciation
- Don't eat while walking in public
- Cover your mouth when using toothpicks
- Don't stick chopsticks upright in rice (resembles funeral rites)
- Don't pass food chopstick to chopstick (also resembles funeral rites)
- Pouring drinks for others is customary (not for yourself)

## Religion and Spirituality
- Most Japanese practice both Shinto and Buddhism without conflict
- Religious practices are often cultural rather than deeply spiritual
- New Year (Shogatsu) is the most important holiday
- Visiting shrines and temples for festivals and special occasions is common
- Funeral rites are typically Buddhist, weddings often Shinto or Christian

## Education
- Extremely competitive education system
- High value placed on academic achievement
- "Exam hell" (juken jigoku) describes intense university entrance exam period
- Cram schools (juku) are common for supplementary education
- School uniforms are standard
- Respect for teachers is strong

## Things to Avoid
- Don't point at people (use open hand if needed)
- Don't blow your nose in public (especially at dining table)
- Don't talk loudly on trains or in public spaces
- Don't enter tatami rooms with shoes or slippers
- Don't discuss WWII, atomic bombs, or controversial political topics casually
- Don't hug or touch people you don't know well (personal space is important)
- Don't open gifts in front of the giver
