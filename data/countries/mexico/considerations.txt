# CULTURAL CONSIDERATIONS FOR MEXICO

## Social Behavior
- Mexicans are generally warm, hospitable, and family-oriented
- Personal relationships are highly valued; trust must be built over time
- Formality and respect are important, especially with elders and authority figures
- Use of titles (Licenciado, Doctor, Ingeniero) is common in professional settings
- Close physical proximity during conversation is normal among friends

## Daily Life & Schedule
- Breakfast can be light or heavy, lunch is the main meal (2-4 PM)
- Dinner is usually lighter and eaten around 8-9 PM
- Sunday family gatherings are a strong tradition
- Social life varies by city: urban areas are more active at night
- Many businesses close for lunch (comida) in smaller towns

## Communication Style
- Indirect communication is common to maintain harmony and avoid confrontation
- "Ahorita" (right now) can mean anything from immediately to never
- Saying "no" directly is often avoided; watch for non-verbal cues
- Using diminutives (-ito, -ita) is very common to show affection or soften requests
- Humor and informal language are common once trust is established

## Work Culture
- Relationships and personal connections (palancas) are often crucial for opportunities
- Hierarchy is respected; decisions come from the top
- Work-life balance is valued but long hours are common
- Personal favors and loyalty are important in professional relationships
- Formality decreases as relationships strengthen

## Social Etiquette
- Greetings are important: handshakes for men, kiss on cheek for women
- Always greet everyone when entering a room or gathering
- Being late (30 min) for social events is often acceptable
- Splitting bills is less common; one person usually pays
- Tipping is expected (10-15% in restaurants, varies for other services)

## Food & Drink
- Food is central to Mexican culture and identity
- Meals are social occasions, not rushed
- Street food is a way of life and socially acceptable at all economic levels
- Sharing food is a sign of trust and friendship
- Traditional foods vary greatly by region
- Mezcal and tequila are cultural symbols, but consumption varies

## Regional Differences
- Strong regional identities and accents exist (Norteños, Chilangos, Jarochos, etc.)
- Coastal areas are more relaxed, northern states more direct and business-oriented
- Central/Southern regions maintain more indigenous traditions
- Border cities have unique culture influenced by proximity to the US
- Each state has distinct foods, slang, and customs

## Family & Religion
- Family is the core social unit; extended family is very important
- Catholicism deeply influences culture even among non-practicing individuals
- Quinceañeras, weddings, and baptisms are major social events
- Respect for parents and elders is fundamental
- Day of the Dead (Día de Muertos) is a major cultural tradition, not morbid

## Things to Avoid
- Don't confuse Mexican Spanish with other Latin American variants
- Avoid discussing politics, corruption, or drug cartels unless you know the person well
- Don't assume all Mexicans eat spicy food or wear sombreros
- Avoid stereotypes about laziness or siestas
- Don't bring up the US-Mexico relationship or immigration issues casually
- Never use derogatory slang or make jokes about indigenous heritage
