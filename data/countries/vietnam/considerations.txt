# CULTURAL CONSIDERATIONS FOR VIETNAM

## Social Behavior
- Vietnamese culture places extreme importance on respect for elders and family hierarchy
- Age and seniority are highly valued - always address older people with appropriate titles (anh, chị, ông, bà)
- Saving face (mất mặt) is crucial - avoid public confrontation or causing embarrassment
- Modesty and humility are valued virtues - boasting is seen negatively
- Close physical proximity with same-sex friends is normal and acceptable
- Indirect communication is preferred over direct confrontation

## Family Structure
- Family is the cornerstone of Vietnamese society
- Multi-generational households are common, especially in rural areas
- Filial piety is fundamental - children are expected to care for aging parents
- Family decisions are often collective rather than individual
- Extended family maintains close relationships and regular contact
- Ancestral worship is practiced in most households

## Daily Life & Schedule
- Early risers: many Vietnamese wake up around 5-6 AM
- Breakfast is important and often eaten outside at street food stalls
- Lunch is typically 11:30 AM - 1:00 PM
- Dinner around 6-7 PM, often a family gathering
- Evening walks and socializing at cafes are popular
- Siesta (nghỉ trưa) is common in some workplaces

## Communication Style
- Indirect communication to maintain harmony and avoid conflict
- Smiling can mean many things - agreement, disagreement, embarrassment, or nervousness
- Saying "no" directly is considered rude - expect indirect refusals
- Personal questions (age, income, marital status) are normal and not considered invasive
- Using both hands when giving or receiving items shows respect
- Pointing with fingers is considered rude - use whole hand

## Work Culture
- Hierarchy and seniority are very important in workplace
- Personal relationships (quan hệ) matter more than strict rules
- Face-to-face meetings preferred over email for important matters
- Networking and connections are crucial for business success
- Work-life boundaries can be fluid
- Overtime is common in many industries

## Social Etiquette
- Remove shoes when entering homes and some establishments
- Dress modestly, especially when visiting temples or rural areas
- Table manners: wait for elders to start eating first
- Two hands when giving or receiving business cards or gifts
- Never touch someone's head - it's considered the most sacred part
- Feet are considered the lowest part - don't point feet at people or Buddha images

## Food & Drink
- Coffee culture is huge - Vietnamese coffee (cà phê sữa đá) is a daily ritual
- Street food is integral to Vietnamese culture and very safe to eat
- Sharing food is customary - dishes are placed in center for everyone
- Tea is offered as a sign of hospitality
- Finishing all food on plate can imply host didn't provide enough
- Chopstick etiquette: don't stick them upright in rice (resembles incense at funerals)

## Regional Differences
- North (Hà Nội): more conservative, traditional, reserved
- Central (Huế, Đà Nẵng): known for spicy food and unique dialect
- South (Sài Gòn): more relaxed, entrepreneurial, open to change
- Dialect differences can be significant between regions
- Food preferences vary greatly by region
- Historical rivalry between North and South still influences culture

## Religion & Spirituality
- Buddhism mixed with Confucianism, Taoism, and ancestor worship
- Temples and pagodas are important community centers
- Lunar calendar is used for important dates and festivals
- Feng shui (phong thủy) influences many decisions
- Superstitions play a role in daily life
- Tet (Lunar New Year) is the most important holiday

## Things to Avoid
- Don't criticize Ho Chi Minh or the Communist Party in public
- Avoid discussing the Vietnam War unless the other person brings it up
- Don't confuse Vietnam with other Asian countries
- Never compare Vietnam unfavorably to China
- Don't assume everyone speaks English - Vietnamese is the primary language
- Avoid excessive public displays of affection
- Don't use your left hand alone when giving something (considered unclean)
- Never call Vietnam "Saigon" when referring to the whole country

## Modern Vietnamese Culture
- Rapid modernization, especially in cities like Hồ Chí Minh City and Hà Nội
- Young generation is tech-savvy and globally connected
- Motorbikes are the primary mode of transportation
- Social media is extremely popular (Facebook, Zalo, TikTok)
- Growing middle class with increasing purchasing power
- Traditional values coexist with modern lifestyles
